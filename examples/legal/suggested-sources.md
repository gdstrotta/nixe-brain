# Suggested Sources — Legal Knowledge Base

## Tier 1 — Ingest Immediately

**Active cases and matters**
Your own procedural documents for active litigation, administrative proceedings,
or advisory matters. Case summaries, key filings, procedural timelines.
*These generate the highest-signal wiki content because they reflect your actual work.*

**Anchor legislation**
The primary statutes, codes, and regulations for your practice areas.
Paste the key articles and sections — not the entire text.

**Key precedents / case law**
Binding decisions and leading cases in your areas. The headnotes and ratio decidendi,
not necessarily the full decision.

---

## Tier 2 — Ingest Early

**Institutional actors**
Court websites, regulatory agency guidelines, bar association rules.
Anything that defines the operating environment of your practice.

**Standard forms and templates**
Your own model pleadings, standard contract clauses, recurring document structures.

**Procedural rules**
Court rules, administrative procedures, filing deadlines and requirements
for each jurisdiction where you practice.

---

## Tier 3 — Ingest Progressively

**Doctrine and commentary**
Articles, treatises, and secondary sources on your key legal questions.
Prioritize authors and publications you actively cite.

**Continuing education materials**
CLE materials, webinar notes, conference presentations on evolving areas.

**Client-facing documents**
Anonymized versions of opinions, advice letters, and memos you've produced.
*(Remove identifying information before ingesting.)*

---

## What NOT to Ingest

- Generic law school textbooks (too broad, too foundational)
- Wikipedia articles on legal topics (too general)
- Full text of long statutes (paste key provisions only)
- Superseded legislation or overruled cases (unless specifically tracking evolution)

---

## Suggested First Wave (5 sources)

1. Your two or three most active current matters (procedural summary + key facts)
2. The single most important statute in your primary practice area (key articles)
3. The single most important leading case in your primary practice area
4. Your firm's/department's standard procedures or workflow
5. One recent article or analysis directly relevant to a current matter
